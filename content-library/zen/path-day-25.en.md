## Original Passage

Zhaozhou asked a monk: "Have you been here before?"

The monk said: "Yes, I have been here before."

Zhaozhou said: "Go drink tea."

He asked another monk: "Have you been here before?"

That monk said: "No, I have not been here before."

Zhaozhou said: "Go drink tea."

The head monk asked: "Why do you say 'go drink tea' to the one who has been here, and also 'go drink tea' to the one who has not been here?"

Zhaozhou said: "Head monk."

The head monk responded: "Yes?"

Zhaozhou said: "Go drink tea."

## Modern Reading

The man who has experience: go drink tea. The man who has no experience: go drink tea. The man asking why the answer is the same in both cases: go drink tea. Zhaozhou is not being evasive. He is being precise.

## Commentary

It would be convenient if the teaching changed based on who you are. Those with experience receive one instruction; beginners receive another; those with philosophical sophistication get something more refined.

Zhaozhou does not cooperate with this arrangement.

The head monk's question is the most revealing moment. He has noticed something that looks like a contradiction. He wants an explanation. He receives the same three words that everyone else received.

Go drink tea.

This is not irony. It is the most direct thing Zhaozhou could say. Whatever you bring to this moment — years of practice, your first day, your clever philosophical puzzlement — the moment is the same moment. The tea is the same tea. The sitting down, the cup, the heat, the swallowing — available equally to the experienced and the inexperienced, to the confused and the serene.

There is an implicit rebuke to the head monk, but it is a kind one: you are looking for a difference that will explain things, and in that search you are missing the tea.

Whatever your history with practice, whatever you have understood or failed to understand across these 25 days — today, right now, there is a cup of something waiting. Drink it.
