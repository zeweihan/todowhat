## Original Passage

*Fresh translation from Classical Chinese.*

A monk asked Zhaozhou: "Does a dog have Buddha-nature?"

Zhaozhou said: "Mu."

---

*Wumen's Commentary:*
For the practice of Zen, you must pass the barrier of the patriarchs. For supreme awakening, you must cut off the road of ordinary thinking. If you have not passed the barrier, if you have not cut off the road — then you are a ghost clinging to grass and trees.

What is the barrier of the patriarchs? It is just this one word: Mu. It is the single barrier of the Zen school.

Pick it up. Work on it around the clock. Do not interpret it as nothingness. Do not interpret it as existence or non-existence. It must be like swallowing a red-hot iron ball — you cannot spit it out no matter how hard you try.

---

*Wumen's Verse:*
Dog — Buddha-nature.
The full presentation of the truth.
The moment you add "has" or "has not"
you lose your life.

## Modern Reading

Day 1 of this path opened with the Platform Sutra's declaration that all sentient beings possess Buddha-nature. Now Zhaozhou is asked directly: does a dog have it? And he says: Mu. Not yes, not no. Not "the question doesn't apply." Just: Mu. The word itself — in Chinese, 無, meaning "no" or "nothing" — is not the answer. It is the barrier.

## Commentary

Every intellectual approach to this koan eventually loops back to the same place: you cannot think your way through it. Yes leads somewhere. No leads somewhere. "The question is a category error" leads somewhere. All of those paths are roads of ordinary thinking, and Wumen says plainly: cut off the road of ordinary thinking. Whatever response arises when you pick up Mu and hold it — that response is not Mu.

What Zhaozhou gave us is not a philosophical position. He was not making a claim about ontology. He spoke one syllable and left the room. The monk who received it would have carried it for weeks, months, years — not analyzing it, but holding it the way you hold a stone that's grown warm in your hand. Not asking what it means. Asking: what is the thing that is asking?

There is a particular quality of attention that arises when you stop trying to resolve a question and simply stay with it. Not passive. Not defeated. Alive to the not-knowing. That quality is what Zhaozhou was pointing at. Mu is not the answer. Mu is the invitation to find the one who asks.
