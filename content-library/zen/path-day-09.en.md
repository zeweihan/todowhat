## Original Passage

*Fresh translation from Classical Chinese.*

A monk asked Master Yunmen: "When the tree withers and the leaves fall — what then?"

Yunmen said: "Body exposed in the golden wind."

---

Xuedou's verse:

> It raises and it falls, the question of a lifetime.  
> The golden wind — has it not been blowing all along?  
> The waves of the Caoxi River lap back and forth.  
> Who speaks of flowers? Who speaks of falling?

## Modern Reading

Autumn arrives. The tree does not resist. The leaves, which clung all spring and summer, release without apology. The monk's question is not really about trees. He is asking: when everything I depend on is stripped away — when the conditions that supported me dissolve — what remains?

Yunmen does not answer with consolation. He offers something colder and more useful: the body stands in the wind. Nothing is hidden. Whatever you are, that is what the wind finds.

## Commentary

The monk is asking about loss. About the moment when the structures fall — a role, a relationship, a way of understanding yourself that held for years. When the leaves of that go, what is left?

Yunmen says: body exposed in the golden wind.

Not: something sacred survives. Not: the essential self endures. He says: you stand there. In the wind. With everything that covered you now on the ground.

This is not a comfort. It is a description. And Yunmen would say the description is already more than you need.

What you cling to most is not a thing. It is the shape of yourself. The idea that, beneath the changing conditions, there is a stable form that all of this is happening to. The leaves fall and the tree is still there. The tree remains.

But look at the tree in winter. What you called the tree — was that the leaves?

Sit with the question the monk asked. Not as philosophy. As an invitation to feel what you are gripping.

## Socratic Prompts

1. When you imagine losing the role or status that currently defines you — the job, the title, the position in the family — what do you fear would be revealed about you once that covering is gone?

2. Yunmen does not say the body is protected or sustained. He says it is *exposed*. What would change in your daily life if you stopped trying to manage how much of yourself others see?
