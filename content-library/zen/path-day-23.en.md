## Original Passage

The Master addressed the assembly:

> Good friends, what is it to sit in Zen? In this teaching, there is no obstruction and no hindrance. Outwardly, in the midst of all good and evil situations, not a single thought arising in the mind — this is called sitting. Inwardly, seeing the undisturbed nature of one's original self — this is called Zen.

> Good friends, what is Zen Concentration? Outwardly to be free from form is Zen. Inwardly, not to be thrown into confusion is Concentration. If you cling to outer form, your inner mind will be confused. If you are free from outer form, your inner nature will not be confused. Your original nature is naturally pure and concentrated. It is only because of the contact with situations that confusion arises.

---

The traditional saying attributed to Qingyuan Weixin:

> Before I had studied Zen for thirty years, I saw mountains as mountains and water as water. Then, when I arrived at a more intimate knowledge, I came to see mountains as not mountains, and water as not water. But now that I have understood the very substance of Zen, I see mountains once again as mountains, and water once again as water.

## Modern Reading

Before practice: chop wood, carry water. After practice: chop wood, carry water. The task does not change. What changes — quietly, without announcement — is who is doing it and how they are inside the doing.

## Commentary

Huineng's instruction seems counterintuitive. He is addressing monks who came to him for guidance on meditation, and he is telling them that meditation is not a posture. It is not a room, a cushion, a scheduled hour. It is a quality of mind that either runs through everything or is nowhere at all.

Qingyuan Weixin's three stages are often quoted as a kind of promise. Start here; confusion in the middle; arrive somewhere cleaner. That reading is too linear. What the stages actually describe is the difference between the surface and the substance of an experience.

First seeing: you are chopping wood. The task is what it appears to be.

Second seeing: you begin to understand that "you" and "wood" and "chopping" are all interpretations placed over a raw experience. Disorientation follows.

Third seeing: you are chopping wood. Exactly as before. Except that now you know it from the inside.

The wood does not care which stage you are in. The axe does not know. Whatever you are doing today — washing, walking, typing, cooking — it will not announce when you are in it correctly. That is the difficulty and the gift.
