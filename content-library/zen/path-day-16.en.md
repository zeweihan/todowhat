## Original Passage

*Fresh translation from Classical Chinese.*

Master Ma (馬祖道一) was unwell. The temple superintendent came to inquire: "How is Your Reverence's health these days?"

The Master said: "Sun-face Buddha. Moon-face Buddha."

---

*Note on the allusion:* The Sutra of Buddha Names records that Sun-face Buddha (日面佛) has a lifespan of one thousand eight hundred years. Moon-face Buddha (月面佛) lives for one day and one night.

---

Xuedou's verse:

> Sun-face Buddha, Moon-face Buddha —  
> What kind of people were the ancient emperors?  
> For twenty years I have suffered.  
> How many times I descended, how many times I rose.  
> The struggle is just this.  
> Do you understand, or not?

## Modern Reading

A master is dying. His body is failing. A student asks the ritual question: how are you? And instead of "poorly" or "at peace" or "this body is impermanent," he names two Buddhas — one who lives nearly two thousand years, one who lives a single day. Both at once. He is not choosing between them. He is not consoling himself with the long life or resigning himself to the short. He is pointing at something that contains both without preferring either.

## Commentary

Master Ma is not well. His body knows it. The superintendent knows it. And instead of discussing it — instead of placing this sickness somewhere on a scale from recovery to death — he names two Buddhas who live in entirely different orders of time.

Sun-face Buddha: eighteen hundred years. Moon-face Buddha: one day and one night.

He is not saying "I will live long." He is not saying "I am ready to die." He is saying: right here, in this sickroom, both are present. The vast span and the single day. Neither is more real. Neither is to be preferred.

This is not resignation. It is not optimism. It is something much more difficult — the refusal to leave the present moment by measuring it against a timeline you prefer.

Your life is also both. The breath you are drawing right now is one day and one night long. And it is eighteen hundred years long. It will not be more present if you live to ninety. It will not be less complete if you do not.

What does it feel like to stop measuring this moment against a future one?

## Socratic Prompts

1. You are sitting here right now, in a specific room, in a body that has a specific history of aches and ease. Without evaluating it — what does this body feel like from the inside, at this exact moment?
2. Master Ma doesn't explain the two Buddhas. He just names them. What happens in you when someone refuses to explain, and just points?
3. Think of something in your life you are waiting to feel good about — a project, a relationship, a version of yourself you are building toward. How much of your current moment is being spent waiting for that to arrive?
4. "Sun-face Buddha. Moon-face Buddha." If someone asked you right now how you are — really — what two things could you name that capture both the vast and the immediate?
5. The superintendent asked a routine question. Ma answered from somewhere else entirely. When did you last receive a question and answer from a completely different layer than the one the question came from?
6. This session will end. You will move on to the rest of your day. Between now and then — in the minutes of actual practice you have left — what are you willing to put down in order to be here?
7. Ma was sick. He was still the teacher. Is there something in your life right now that you believe disqualifies you from being fully present — too tired, too distracted, too behind? What if that condition is the teaching, not the obstacle?
