## Original Passage

*Fresh translation from Classical Chinese.*

Linji Yixuan addressed the assembly:

> Followers of the Way, if you want to see the dharma as it truly is, do not be deceived by others — whether within or without, whatever you meet, slay it. If you meet the Buddha, slay the Buddha. If you meet the patriarchs, slay the patriarchs. If you meet the arhats, slay the arhats. If you meet your parents, slay your parents. If you meet your kinsmen, slay your kinsmen. Only then will you find liberation. Only then, by not clinging to anything, will you pass through freely.

And again:

> The Buddha is a phantom of purity. The patriarchs are old monks with broken sandals. Are you not yet free?

## Modern Reading

Linji is not recommending violence. He is recommending the end of something more difficult to relinquish than anything physical: the images you carry inside you as authorities. The Buddha you visualize as an object of reverence. The teacher whose approval organizes your practice. The idea of "the right way to do this." Linji says: cut that. Whatever concept has set itself up as the destination — that is the thing to look at.

## Commentary

This is the most dangerous teaching in Zen. And Linji knew it.

The people in that hall had given years to this. They had left families, shaved heads, memorized sutras, sat through winters. They were attached — deeply attached — to the project of attaining something. They were attached to the Buddha as goal. To the master as authority. To the dharma as map.

Linji cuts through all of it in one gesture. If you meet the Buddha, he says, slay him.

Understand what that means. The Buddha you meet on the road is not the awakened Shakyamuni. It is your own concept of awakening — your mental image of what arriving looks like. And that image is the obstacle. The seeker who is attached to arriving cannot arrive, because arriving would require giving up the identity of the seeker.

This is the sharpest edge of non-attachment: it includes the practice itself. It includes your relationship to Zen. It includes this app, this session, this moment of feeling like you are making progress.

Put it down. Not because it lacks value. Because clinging to it stops the very thing it was pointing toward.

## Socratic Prompts

1. What does your mental image of an "enlightened person" look like? Be honest about the image — the qualities, the comportment, the sense of arrival. Now ask: in what ways is your practice organized around becoming that image?

2. If your spiritual practice were quietly giving you an identity as a "serious practitioner" — would you want to know? What would you be willing to release?
