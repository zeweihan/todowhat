## Original Passage

*Fresh translation from Classical Chinese.*

Yunmen said: "The world is vast and wide. Why do you put on your seven-fold robe at the sound of the bell?"

---

Wumen's commentary:

*Fresh translation from Classical Chinese.*

> When studying Zen, you must not fall into sound and form. Even so, tell me — at the sound of the bell, before the monk reaches for the robe, what is that? Does it arrive before the bell, with the bell, or after? If you say before — the bell has not yet rung. If you say after — the motion has already begun. If you say with — then you and the bell are the same thing. Tell me: where is the practice?

Wumen's verse:

> One who realizes the Way — it is like this.  
> One who does not realize the Way — it is also like this.  
> Not knowing is nearest.  
> Knowing is farthest.

## Modern Reading

Day 17 asked who hears the bell. Today the question moves earlier: what is your mind in the split second before the bell sounds? Not after the recognition. Not in the response. In the silence that precedes the event — pure attention, collected, not yet triggered, not yet named. This is 专注 (zhuānzhù): focused, gathered, present without object. Before the bell, after yesterday, before tomorrow. This single breath, this single still moment. This is where the week ends.

## Commentary

Three weeks. Twenty-one sessions.

This case has appeared before in a different light. On Day 17, the bell sounded and you were asked who heard it. Today the bell has not yet rung.

You are in that pause.

Yunmen's question is about the gap between stimulus and response. The monk hears the bell and puts on the robe — automatic, correct, trained. Between the sound and the motion, there is a moment so brief that almost no one notices it. Pure attention, not yet organized into recognition. Not yet sorted into a response. Not yet filed under any category.

Wumen presses: before, during, or after the bell? Any answer separates you from the thing itself.

This is where three weeks of practice has been leading. Not to a conclusion. Not to a state of permanent clarity. To the ability to notice this gap — the one that was always there — and rest in it for a moment before the robe goes on, before the response forms, before the day resumes.

The bell will ring in a moment. Your day will continue. Before it does — here you are. This breath. This silence. This.

That is the whole teaching.

## Socratic Prompts

1. Right now, before you have done anything with this session — before you have evaluated it, described it, or decided what to take from it — what is simply present? Not what you think. What is here.
2. Between the moment you wake up and the moment you reach for your phone, there is a gap. How wide is it for you? Have you ever stayed in it deliberately? What is it like?
3. Think of a decision you are carrying right now — something unresolved. Before you think about the options, before you analyze — in the moment just before the thinking begins, what do you feel in your body?
4. Three weeks of practice. Not as a record or an achievement — as a lived fact. What has changed in the texture of your days that you would not have predicted? Be specific. Name one thing.
5. Yunmen says the world is vast and wide. You are one person, in one room, with one body. What does it feel like to hold both of those facts simultaneously — the vastness and the absolute specificity of this?
6. You have reached the end of Week 3. One week remains. What are you carrying into it that you could put down — not a habit to fix, not a goal to abandon, but a weight that has been unnecessary all along?
7. The bell is about to ring. Your robe is within reach. In the silence before the sound — this exact silence, right now — are you here?
