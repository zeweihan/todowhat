## Original Passage

Huineng addressed the assembly:

> Good friends, 'no-abiding' is the fundamental nature of human beings. In the successive moments of thought, they do not dwell on anything. Past thought, present thought, future thought — thought follows thought without interruption. But if for one thought there is a cessation, the dharma body separates from the physical body.
>
> In the midst of all phenomena, thought does not abide anywhere. If thought abides, it is bondage. If no thought abides anywhere in the midst of the ten thousand dharmas — that is liberation.

And again:

> That which has no abiding in any place — that is the root.

## Modern Reading

Not: empty the mind. Not: refuse to think. Not: achieve a condition of blankness.

What Huineng is pointing at is harder and more ordinary than any of those. He is pointing at something happening right now in the nature of thought itself — each one already gone before the next arrives. The problem is not thought. The problem is the nail you pound in to make one of them stay.

## Commentary

You do not hold a thought. You only believe you do.

Look carefully at what actually happens when a thought arises. It appears. And then — whether you intend it or not — it is gone. Another has taken its place. This has been happening every moment since you were born. You have never, not once, successfully made a thought stay.

What you have done is build a story around one thought and carried that story forward — which is a different thing. The thought itself departed. You kept the story.

Huineng calls no-abiding the *root*. Not a technique. Not a state to achieve. The root — meaning this is what the mind already is, before you intervene.

The practice today is not to stop thoughts from abiding. It is to notice that they already do not. Sit with the ordinary fact of the mind's own freedom. It has always been moving. You are the one who decided otherwise.

## Socratic Prompts

1. There is something you have decided about yourself that you have been carrying for years — a wound, a strength, a fixed verdict. What would it mean if that conclusion were simply a thought that stayed past its natural lifespan?

2. When you sit with a difficult emotion and wait — really wait — what do you notice happens to it on its own, without your interference?
