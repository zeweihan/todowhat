## Original Passage

*Fresh translation from Classical Chinese.*

Good friends, the self-nature of Bodhi is originally pure. The ten thousand dharmas arise within self-nature itself. When you think evil, evil action follows. When you think wholesome, wholesome action follows. In this way all dharmas are contained within this one nature — and yet the nature itself is not stained by any of them. Good friends: were you to see clearly into your own self-nature, that alone would be sufficient.

## Modern Reading

The Platform Sutra opens not with doctrine but with a single claim that precedes all instruction: what you are searching for is already present. Buddha-nature is not a state to be achieved. It is not the result of study or practice. It is what you are, prior to everything else. The searching itself may be the thing in the way.

## Commentary

Huineng grew up illiterate, earning his living as a firewood seller in the south. He had no formal training and had never entered a monastery. One afternoon, passing through a market, he heard a verse from the Diamond Sutra recited aloud — and something in him opened. He went north to find the Fifth Patriarch and was put to work in the kitchen, pounding rice. He never entered the lecture hall. When the moment came to transmit the robe and bowl — the symbols of lineage — it was Huineng who received them, not the learned senior monks.

This is where the Platform Sutra begins: with that story, and with a man who had nothing to recommend him except that he had seen directly. The teaching he gives is not a system. It is a single insistence: your nature is already pure. Not pure as a moral condition. Pure as in: unobstructed. Whatever clouds it — anxious thought, accumulated habit, the effort to be otherwise — is not the nature itself. This is not reassurance. It is a challenge to look.
