## Original Passage

*Fresh translation from Classical Chinese.*

Emperor Wu of Liang asked the great teacher Bodhidharma: "What is the first principle of the holy teaching?"

Bodhidharma replied: "Vast emptiness — nothing holy."

The Emperor said: "Who stands before me?"

Bodhidharma replied: "I don't know."

## Modern Reading

Emperor Wu had built monasteries. Funded translations. Supported thousands of monks. He came to Bodhidharma wanting confirmation — some accounting of the merit he had earned. Bodhidharma gave him something harder: the floor fell out from under the entire framework. No holy. No ledger. And no fixed self to be doing the asking.

## Commentary

The Emperor had done everything right. Built monasteries. Funded the copying of sutras. Supported monks across the kingdom. By any reasonable measure, he was a great patron of the dharma.

He wanted Bodhidharma to confirm this. To tell him where he stood.

Bodhidharma's answer landed like a stone into still water.

Vast emptiness. Nothing holy.

Not: you have great merit. Not: the merit concept is complicated. Just — there is no ledger. The framework in which merit accumulates, in which sainthood can be measured and reported, is not the final framework.

The Emperor tried again with a simpler question: then who is this, standing before me?

Bodhidharma said: I don't know.

Not as a riddle. Not as performance. As a simple and direct statement from someone who had looked very carefully at the one who might answer and found — no fixed thing there to point to.

These two exchanges contain the whole architecture of Zen: nothing holy, no fixed self. Everything else is commentary. The Emperor, unable to receive this, sent Bodhidharma away. Bodhidharma crossed the river. The teaching remained.

Before you speak today, before you reach for a word — what is already there?
