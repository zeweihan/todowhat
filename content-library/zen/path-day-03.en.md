## Original Passage

*Fresh translation from Classical Chinese.*

Emperor Wu of Liang asked the great master Bodhidharma: "What is the highest sacred truth?"

Bodhidharma said: "Vast emptiness — nothing sacred."

The Emperor said: "Who is this, standing before me?"

Bodhidharma said: "I don't know."

The Emperor did not understand. Bodhidharma crossed the Yangtze River and went to the Kingdom of Wei.

Later, the Emperor asked the National Teacher Zhizhi about this encounter. Zhizhi said: "Your Majesty, do you know who that man was?" The Emperor said: "I don't know." Zhizhi said: "That was the Bodhisattva Avalokiteshvara, transmitting the Buddha's mind-seal." The Emperor felt regret and wished to send a messenger to invite Bodhidharma back. Zhizhi said: "Your Majesty, even if you sent the whole kingdom after him, he would not return."

## Modern Reading

Emperor Wu had built temples and supported monks throughout his reign. He expected a ledger of merit and a confirmation of his spiritual standing. Bodhidharma gave him neither. "Vast emptiness — nothing sacred" is not nihilism; it is the view from which all merit and all sacredness arise. The Emperor reached for a category — "who are you?" — and got a silence that had no place to land.

## Commentary

There is a version of spiritual life that is fundamentally transactional. You perform meritorious acts — charitable giving, correct behavior, sustained practice — and you expect a return in the form of progress, standing, or eventual freedom. Emperor Wu had been operating exactly this way, and doing it more visibly than almost anyone in his kingdom. He had reason to feel he was doing well. When Bodhidharma arrived, the Emperor was presenting his credentials.

Bodhidharma said nothing useful back. "Vast emptiness — nothing sacred" is not a dismissal. It is an accurate description of the view from the other side of the transaction. From that vantage, there is no balance sheet. There is no merit and no debtor. There is no distinguished teacher and no important emperor. There is no "sacred" category separate from the ordinary, because everything that exists is already exactly what it is.

The Emperor missed it. He wanted a map, and was given the territory. He then asked: who is this person who speaks this way? Bodhidharma said: I don't know. This "I don't know" is not evasion. It is the same answer, said twice.

## Socratic Prompts

1. When you look at your own life's activities — work, relationships, practice — do you find yourself keeping score? What would it mean to act without expecting the score to matter?
2. *Koan: "Vast emptiness — nothing sacred." — Right now, where is the sacred?*
