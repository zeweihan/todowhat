## Original Passage

The Fifth Patriarch Hongren called Huineng to him in the night, when the other monks were sleeping. He explained the Diamond Sutra until Huineng was fully awakened. Then:

> The Patriarch said: "From ancient times the transmission of the dharma has been as delicate as a hanging thread. If you stay here, some will harm you. You must leave at once."
>
> Huineng asked: "Which direction shall I go?"
>
> The Patriarch said: "Stop at Huai. Settle at Hui."
>
> Then, at midnight, the Patriarch transmitted the robe and the bowl, symbols of the succession, and said: "You are now the Sixth Patriarch. Take the robe as evidence of the dharma. Pass it on with care. As for the dharma itself — transmit mind with mind. Let no one be confused."
>
> He added: "Since ancient times, the transmission of life has been as fine as a thread. If you carry the robe, others will fight over it. Stop the transmission of the robe here. Your own mind is the Buddha. Do not doubt it."

## Modern Reading

The robe is silk and thread. The dharma is not silk and thread. The Fifth Patriarch knows what will happen if the robe travels north: monks will follow it, and the following will become fighting. So he stops the material lineage at Huineng and tells him: from here, transmit mind with mind. No object. No visible token. Just the living fact of awakening, passed from one open awareness to another.

## Commentary

Consider what Hongren is doing.

He has spent his life inside a system of transmission — robe to student, bowl to student, lineage made visible by objects. He is the keeper of that system. And at midnight, alone with Huineng, he breaks the chain. He hands over the robe and says: stop here. Let the object die.

This is not a small decision. For a teacher trained in the importance of lineage, this costs something. The robe is how the tradition knows itself. He is releasing that.

What he does instead is harder. He asks Huineng to trust a transmission that leaves no evidence. No robe. No bowl. Only the certainty of what happened in that room. Only the direct recognition of mind.

We are attached to the objects of our practice. The cushion in the right position. The book with our notes in the margins. The teacher whose name we can cite. These things are not the enemy. But when the object becomes the container for what we believe — when the container is what we are actually protecting — then Hongren's instruction applies to us too.

The robe stopped here. What did not stop?

## Socratic Prompts

1. What physical objects, routines, or external structures are you currently treating as necessary conditions for your practice to be real? What would remain if those were taken away?

2. Hongren transmits the dharma in secret, in the dark, with instructions to flee. The living teaching had to leave in order to survive. Is there something you are protecting so carefully that you have stopped letting it move?
