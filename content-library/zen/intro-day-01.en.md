## Original Passage

Shenxiu, the head monk, wrote a verse on the wall of the south corridor:

> Our body is the Bodhi-tree,  
> And our mind a mirror bright.  
> Carefully we wipe them hour by hour  
> And let no dust alight.

When Huineng — who could not read — heard the verse read aloud, he asked a brother to write beside it:

> There is no Bodhi-tree,  
> Nor stand of a mirror bright.  
> Since all is void,  
> Where can the dust alight?

## Modern Reading

Two monks in the same monastery, one teaching. The first: tend the mind with care; polish it daily; clear away what clouds it. The second: there is no mirror. There is no dust. There is no surface on which anything could settle. If both monks are pointing at something true, what does that make the truth?

## Commentary

Two verses. One monastery. One night.

Shenxiu wrote what any careful, serious person would write. Tend the mind like a surface. Keep it clear. Do the daily work of removing what clouds it. For most people who come to practice, this feels exactly right.

Huineng's verse goes the other direction entirely. No tree. No mirror. No dust. This is not a rejection of Shenxiu's sincerity. It is a different question altogether.

Shenxiu asks: how do I maintain my mind?

Huineng asks: what is this thing you are calling a mind?

The Fifth Patriarch, reading Huineng's verse in the torchlight, recognized something. He rubbed it out with his sandal the next morning — so no one would fight over it — and then passed the robe and bowl to Huineng in secret, in the dark.

What he transmitted was not a position in a debate. It was a question. The kind that does not dissolve when you think about it long enough. The kind that opens.

Carry that question into your sitting today. Not to answer it. Just to hold it.

## Socratic Prompts

1. You have spent years attending to your mental state — calming it, improving it, managing it. What if the mind you have been tending is not what you think it is? What would that change about how you practice?
