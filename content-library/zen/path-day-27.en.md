## Original Passage

Fayan of Qingliang asked two senior monks: "You, monks — roll up the blind."

Both monks rolled up the blind in the same way.

Fayan said: "One gains, one loses."

---

Wumen's commentary:

> Tell me — who gains and who loses? If you have an eye for this, you will see where Qingliang's teacher failed. However, I strongly caution you against discussing gain and loss.

Wumen's verse:

> When the blind is rolled up, the great sky opens wide.  
> But the great sky does not accord with Zen.  
> Best to throw the great sky away entirely —  
> And not let a single breath of wind enter.

---

*The traditional teaching on the finger and moon:*

The Zen teaching says: do not mistake the finger pointing at the moon for the moon itself. When the finger is perfectly still and the direction is exact, you can be so absorbed in the precision of the pointing that you forget to look.

## Modern Reading

Two monks. Same movement. One gains, one loses. Watching from outside, you cannot tell which is which — and that is the point.

## Commentary

The blind rolls up the same way whether you are inside the act or narrating it. The motion is identical. The hands move the same. But Fayan sees something — a difference in how each monk is inhabiting what they are doing — and says so plainly: one gains, one loses.

What he is not doing is offering a judgment about technique. He is not correcting their form. He is noticing whether they are actually there.

The finger-and-moon teaching belongs here. A finger pointing at the moon is useful precisely as long as you use it to find the moon. The moment you study the finger, analyze its angle, appreciate the elegance of the pointing — the moon has gone. But this is also what happens with practice itself. The session is a finger. The daily prompt is a finger. The commentary you are reading right now is a finger.

All pointing.

Wumen's verse is unusually severe: even the great sky that opens when the blind rolls up — throw it away. Don't hold on to the opening. Don't make the vast view into a possession.

Today: roll up the blind. Look at what is there. Leave the blind behind.
