## Original Passage

Before his death, Huineng addressed his closest disciples:

> "Good friends, the dharma has no sudden or gradual. Among human beings some are quick, some are slow. The deluded are taught through gradual practice; the awakened practice the sudden teaching. But to know your own original mind — that is to see your own original nature. Once awakened, there is essentially no difference.
>
> "Without the teaching there would be no transmission. Without the transmission there would be no teaching. But you must not say that I have left you a fixed dharma. What I transmit is the capacity of the nature — the mind ground that can permeate all dharmas. Do not seek the dharma outside the mind. Do not seek the mind outside this moment.
>
> "After I am gone, do not weep and mourn. Those who receive condolences and gifts are not my disciples. All you need to do is to know your own mind and see your own nature."

## Modern Reading

A teacher who spent his life teaching, saying at the end: I have not given you a doctrine. I have not handed you a position to hold. What I gave you was a way of looking. And a way of looking cannot be held, cannot be filed, cannot be protected. It either happens in you, in each moment, or it does not happen at all.

## Commentary

This is the instruction Huineng leaves when there is nothing left to say.

He spent decades watching students do the thing students do: take the teaching and make it solid. Build a system from it. Defend that system. Mistake the system for the thing the teaching was pointing at.

So in the last chapter he says: there is no fixed dharma.

This is not modesty. It is precision. The dharma — the true teaching — is what the mind does when it is not clinging to any formulation of the dharma. It is the space between the words. The moment before you apply the framework you learned to the experience you are having.

Every tradition hardens over time. The living gesture becomes a rule. The rule becomes a doctrine. The doctrine becomes the thing people defend when the living gesture is long forgotten.

Huineng knew this. He had watched Shenxiu — sincere, serious, devoted — still polishing the mirror. He had written the counter-verse. He had received the robe. And now he said: stop transmitting the robe. And: there is no fixed dharma.

What remains when the system falls away? That is the question this week has been building toward.

## Socratic Prompts

1. You have developed a particular view of how practice works — a model of progress, a sense of what counts as a good day and a bad day. How attached are you to being right about that model? What would you lose if the model were wrong?

2. Huineng says do not seek the dharma outside the mind. Where have you been looking for answers that you could only ever find in direct experience?
