## Original Passage

*Fresh translation from Classical Chinese.*

At the end of the summer retreat, Master Suigan said to his assembly:

> "I have been talking to you all summer. Look — do I still have my eyebrows?"

Baoning said: "The robber's heart grows faint."

Wuzu said: "Grown."

Yunmen said: "Barrier."

---

Fu Dashi's verse on the empty hand (空手):

> Empty hands hold a hoe.  
> On foot, I ride a water buffalo.  
> The person crosses the bridge —  
> The bridge flows; the water does not.

## Modern Reading

Suigan asks his students to look at his face. He has spent a summer explaining, parsing, pointing. In the old saying, a teacher who mistakes the conceptual for the real loses his eyebrows — they are burned away by the falseness of his teaching. Has he been genuine? Have his words carried the thing they were pointing at, or only carried themselves?

The three answers from his students are three distinct responses to that question. Fu Dashi's verse asks the same thing in a different direction: what do you hold when you hold nothing? What moves when you stand still?

## Commentary

This is the last session of the week. Seven days on non-attachment. And now the question turns around and faces you.

What have you been doing with these teachings?

If you have been collecting them — adding them to a store of understanding, building a more sophisticated relationship with the question of clinging — then you have been clinging to the teaching about clinging. Suigan knows this trap. He has been talking all summer. He asks: did any of this burn the eyebrows?

Fu Dashi's verse is a paradox designed to stop the collecting mind. Empty hands hold a hoe: something is happening, and the hand is also empty. The person crosses the bridge: activity, motion, daily life. The bridge flows; the water does not: the fixed things move, the moving things are still.

This is not a puzzle to solve. It is a picture of what non-attachment looks like when it is lived rather than understood. The practitioner acts fully — holds the hoe, crosses the bridge — without the action crystallizing into an identity, a possession, a record.

You have been working for fourteen days. The question for this closing session is not what you have learned. It is what you are willing to put down, even now, even the learning itself.

Come empty-handed. There is no other way to arrive.

## Socratic Prompts

1. After two weeks of practice, what have you accumulated — insights, frameworks, a sense of how you are doing? Hold that loosely for a moment. If you released your assessment of your own progress, what would be left of the practice?

2. Fu Dashi holds a hoe with empty hands. He works without owning the work. Think of something you do well. Where does the doing stop and the need to be known as someone who does it well begin?
