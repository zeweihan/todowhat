## Original Passage

Langye asked: "The bright and clear original nature inherently exists. Why do mountains, rivers, and the great earth suddenly arise?"

---

Xuedou's verse closing the hundred cases:

> The bright and clear — how can it sustain mountains, rivers, and the earth?  
> A black tortoise walks the dark water, carrying the ancient text.  
> Stop reading so many commentaries.  
> The spring breezes cause the flowers to fall in confusion.

## Modern Reading

The original mind is pristine and complete — so the teaching says. But look around: mountains. Rivers. Your desk. Your body. The noise from the street. All of this arising constantly, thick and insistent. If the original nature is clear, where does all of this come from?

## Commentary

Case 100 is the last case in the Blue Cliff Record. Xuedou chose it carefully. He did not end with a resolution, a triumph, a clear arrival. He ended with a question.

Langye's question is not philosophical. Or rather, it is philosophical in the deepest sense — it is asking about the relationship between the pure and the particular, between the spaciousness that practice opens and the relentless density of actual life.

You have been sitting. You have glimpsed something — a quality of presence, a gap in the noise, a moment of clarity. And then the earth shows up. The errands, the body, the obligations. The mountains did not wait for you to be ready.

This is the koan of ordinary life. The original nature does not hover above the mountains. It is — if it is anywhere — in how the mountain is a mountain. In how the river keeps being a river. In the weight of the earth under your feet while you are carrying water, chopping wood, washing your bowl.

Xuedou's advice is pointed: stop reading so many commentaries. The spring breeze is moving the blossoms right now, and you are indoors, annotating.

Tomorrow is the last session. Before that, you have today — one day before the ending, which is also nothing special. This day, exactly as it is.
