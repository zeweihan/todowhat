## Original Passage

*Fresh translation from Classical Chinese.*

A monk came to Zhaozhou and said: "I have just entered the monastery. Please give me your instruction."

Zhaozhou said: "Have you eaten your rice porridge?"

The monk said: "Yes, I have."

Zhaozhou said: "Then go wash your bowl."

The monk was suddenly enlightened.

## Modern Reading

The monk arrived expecting a teaching in the form of concepts — a doctrine, a method, a transmission. Zhaozhou gave him a task so ordinary it seemed like a dismissal. The bowl needed washing. That was the instruction. The monk who understood this did not hear it as a metaphor or a riddle. He heard it as reality speaking directly.

## Commentary

There is something easy to miss in this exchange: the monk was not dismissed. He was given exactly what he asked for. Zhaozhou's instruction was complete. The teaching was not hidden inside the washing of the bowl like a prize in a cereal box. The washing of the bowl was the teaching, in full, without remainder.

We tend to believe that wisdom is located elsewhere — in a better formulation, a deeper text, a more illuminated teacher. This belief is itself the obstacle. Zhaozhou spent decades at the same monastery, walked the same paths, spoke with thousands of monks. His answers were never spectacular. They were always local, immediate, and exactly proportional to the situation in front of him. A monk is hungry; he eats. He has eaten; he washes. This is not a parable about simplicity. It is a demonstration that reality is already fully present in every ordinary act, and that no further commentary is required.

The question worth sitting with: when you washed a bowl this morning, where were you?

## Socratic Prompts

1. Think of a repeated daily task — making coffee, walking to work, loading a dishwasher. When you do it, where is your attention? What would it mean to do that task as if it were the only thing?
2. *Koan: "Wash your bowl." — Before you move to the next thing today, have you finished this one?*
