## Original Passage

A monk said to Zhaozhou: "I have just entered this monastery. Please give me guidance, Master."

Zhaozhou said: "Have you eaten your morning gruel?"

The monk said: "Yes, I have eaten."

Zhaozhou said: "Then go wash your bowl."

The monk had an insight.

---

Xuedou's verse:

> He makes it clear, he makes it clear.  
> A jade broom sweeps away what?  
> The celestial mirror, polished bright —  
> Still there is dust on it.

## Modern Reading

You came here with a question about the great matter — mind, awakening, the nature of existence. He asked if you ate breakfast. You said yes. He said: wash your bowl.

That is the whole instruction. Nothing else is being held back.

## Commentary

Zhaozhou does not ask the monk to sit in meditation, recite a sutra, or contemplate a koan. He asks if the monk has eaten. Then he tells him what to do next.

This is not a parable. It is not a metaphor in which the bowl is the ego and the washing is spiritual labor. Zhaozhou means wash the bowl.

We spend a great deal of time looking for the teaching somewhere other than where we are. The practice is in another tradition, another book, another retreat. The insight will come when I have read more, sat more, understood more. Meanwhile, the bowl is sitting in the sink.

There is something Zhaozhou does not say that is worth attending to. He does not say: once you wash the bowl, you will be ready to receive teaching. He does not say: after you have washed the bowl, come back and I will tell you something important. The monk washes the bowl. That is where the story ends. That is where the insight is.

Week 4 begins here. Not with grand themes but with a used bowl on a table. You have had your breakfast. You know what to do.
