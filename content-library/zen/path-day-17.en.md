## Original Passage

*Fresh translation from Classical Chinese.*

Yunmen said: "The world is vast and wide. Why do you put on your seven-fold robe at the sound of the bell?"

---

Wumen's commentary:

*Fresh translation from Classical Chinese.*

> When studying Zen, you must not fall into sound and form. Even if you attain upon hearing a sound or seeing a form — that is ordinary. Do you not know that the Zen student rides sound, enters through form, and attains clarity at each thought?
>
> Even granted all this — tell me: does the student hear through the ear or through the ear?

Wumen's verse:

> One who realizes the Way — it is like this.  
> One who does not realize the Way — it is also like this.  
> Not knowing is nearest.  
> Knowing is farthest.

## Modern Reading

The bell rings. You put on your robe. You have done this a thousand times. The body knows what to do before the mind has formed a thought about it. Yunmen's question lands underneath that automatic knowing: why? Not why in a philosophical sense. Why right then, right there — what is the thing that heard, and what is the thing that moved? The world is vast and wide, he says. The bell is one small sound in it. Something in you sorted that sound from all other sounds and responded. What is that something?

## Commentary

You know the bell. You have always known the bell.

Yunmen is not asking you to question whether you should put on the robe. The robe gets put on. That is not the issue. The question cuts under the action entirely: in the moment the bell sounded — before the recognition, before the habit, before even the awareness of hearing — what was there?

This is 不知, not-knowing. Not ignorance. Not confusion. The specific place before the labeling begins.

Every moment in your life has this structure. Sound arrives. Recognition follows. Then response. Then reflection on the response. We live almost entirely in the last two layers. The first — the bare contact between awareness and the world — we pass through without stopping.

Wumen gives it away at the end of his verse: not-knowing is nearest. What you are right now, before you name what you are — that is it.

The Zen teachers are not telling you to stay confused. They are telling you that what you call confusion is actually contact. The thing that doesn't yet know what it is hearing is the thing that is most alive.

What does this room sound like when you are not yet deciding what the sounds mean?

## Socratic Prompts

1. Right now, close your eyes for three breaths and just listen. Open them. What was the first sound you heard — not what it was, but what it felt like before you named it? Can you still access that?
2. Yunmen asks his question to a whole assembly of monks. They all put on their robes every day. None of them could answer. What does it feel like to be asked a question about something you do automatically?
3. Is there an action you perform every day that you are so habituated to that you cannot say what it feels like from the inside? Not what it is — what it actually feels like?
4. Wumen says: "Not knowing is nearest." What is your relationship to not knowing? In your daily life, is not-knowing something you move toward or away from?
5. The bell sounds and you respond. Name one thing in your life that reliably pulls you away from the present moment — a notification, a craving, a worry. In the moment it pulls, is there a gap between the pull and your response? How wide is it?
6. If you could not explain this practice to anyone — not the benefits, not the tradition, not what you are working on — what would remain? What would you still be doing and why?
7. "The world is vast and wide." As you sit here right now, how much of that vastness are you in contact with? What would it take to widen what you can hold without losing the specific detail of this room, this breath, this moment?
