## Original Passage

*Fresh translation from Classical Chinese.*

A monk asked Master Zhaozhou: "Does a dog have Buddha-nature?"

Zhaozhou answered: "Mu."

## Modern Reading

A simple question: does an animal possess what a Buddha realized? Zhaozhou knows the sutras; he knows what they say. He answers anyway with a single syllable — not yes, not no — a sound that stops the question cold. Centuries of monks have sat with that syllable until it was no longer a word at all, but an opening.

## Commentary

Mu.

That is the whole thing. One syllable.

The question behind the koan is real. Buddhist teaching holds that all sentient beings possess Buddha-nature. Zhaozhou knows this. He has read the sutras. He has been a monk his whole life. He answers anyway: Mu.

Not yes. Not no. Not maybe.

The character 無 means without, none, not. But Zhaozhou is not saying the dog lacks Buddha-nature. He is doing something else entirely. He is refusing the frame.

Your mind will want to solve this. It will try yes. It will try no. It will reach for interpretations — perhaps Mu means the question dissolves, perhaps it means form is emptiness, perhaps it means —

Let it try. Watch it try.

What the koan actually asks you to do is to hold Mu so completely, so bodily, that you stop reaching for what it means. And somewhere in that stopping, you find yourself — before the question, before the answer, before the reaching.

Sit with Mu today. Not as a word. Not as a puzzle. As a presence that fills the room.
