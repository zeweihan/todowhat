## Original Passage

Zhaozhou sincerely asked Nanquan: "What is the Way?"

Nanquan said: "Ordinary mind is the Way."

Zhaozhou said: "Then should I turn toward it and seek it?"

Nanquan said: "To turn toward it is to go against it."

Zhaozhou said: "If I do not seek it, how can I know it is the Way?"

Nanquan said: "The Way is not about knowing and not-knowing. Knowing is illusion. Not-knowing is blank indifference. When you truly arrive at the Way of no doubt, it is like the great void — open, spacious, vast. How can it be forced into right and wrong?"

At these words, Zhaozhou had a sudden understanding.

## Modern Reading

What is the Way? The mind you actually have right now — not the one you are working toward, not the one you imagine you will have when you have understood enough. This one.

## Commentary

Zhaozhou was already a serious practitioner when he asked Nanquan this question. He was not a beginner looking for an entry point. He wanted to know the nature of the Way itself.

Ordinary mind is the Way.

This is one of the most used and most misused teachings in Zen. It can sound like permission — whatever I am doing is fine, whatever I am thinking is practice. That is not what Nanquan means.

Ordinary mind is not your habitual mind. It is not the mind full of preferences, scores, and comparisons. It is the mind that has not yet reached for anything — the mind before it decides how it is doing, before it evaluates whether this moment is acceptable.

What makes it ordinary is that it is available in the most ordinary moments. In a Tuesday morning. In traffic. In the gap between one thought and the next.

Zhaozhou's follow-up question is the one most people would ask: if I turn toward it, can I reach it? Nanquan's answer is austere. Turning toward it is the wrong move — not because the direction is wrong but because the turning itself re-creates the subject who is seeking, which is the very distance you are trying to close.

The Way is not elsewhere. It is also not nowhere. It is in the completely unmanaged quality of this moment, available without seeking, lost the instant you go looking.

## Socratic Prompts

1. Not the mind you hope to have someday, not the mind at its best — what is the quality of your mind right now, in this ordinary moment? Can you describe it without improvement, without apology?
