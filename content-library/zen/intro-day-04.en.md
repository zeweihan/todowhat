## Original Passage

From Chapter 6, Huineng addresses the assembly on taking refuge in the Three Gems of one's own nature:

> "Good friends, I advise each of you to take refuge in the Three Gems within your own nature. The Buddha is enlightenment; the Dharma is right teaching; the Sangha is purity.
>
> Taking refuge in the lamp of enlightenment in one's own nature scatters the darkness of delusion. This lamp does not diminish when it lights another lamp. It passes on because it cannot be possessed."

From the Verse of Transmission given by the Fifth Patriarch to Huineng (Chapter 1):

> "The purpose of my coming to this land  
> Is to transmit the teaching and to save deluded beings.  
> One flower opens five petals,  
> And the fruit ripens of itself."

## Modern Reading

One lamp lights another. The first does not diminish. The second did not exist before, and now it does, and its light is the same light. This is how the dharma moves — not through doctrines that can be archived, but through direct meeting, mind to mind. Everything passes. Nothing is kept. And yet the capacity to illuminate continues.

## Commentary

One lamp lights another. The first lamp does not diminish. The second lamp did not exist before, and now it does, and its light is the same light.

This is how the dharma passes — from mind to mind, without words, without ceremony except the ceremony of attention. When the Fifth Patriarch passed the robe and bowl to Huineng in the dark of night, he was not giving him a doctrine to protect. He was giving him his confidence in Huineng's own original nature.

Every flame will go out. This is not a tragedy. The flame knows itself only in burning.

Impermanence in Zen is not something to brace against. It is a fact to meet with open eyes. What we cling to cannot stay. What we release continues in ways we cannot predict or plan.

The lamp of dharma does not belong to any one master, any one generation of monks, any one tradition. It passes because it is not a possession. It moves because it cannot be still.

Sit with this today: not as a philosophy about impermanence, but as something directly in front of you right now. What are you holding tonight that you cannot keep? What would it mean to pass that flame forward, and let the holding go?
