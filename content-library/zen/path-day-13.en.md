## Original Passage

*Fresh translation from Classical Chinese.*

A monk asked Zhaozhou: "What is the meaning of Bodhidharma coming from the West?"

Zhaozhou said: "The cypress tree in the front garden."

---

Wumen's commentary:

> If you clearly understand what Zhaozhou said about the cypress tree, there is no Shakyamuni before you and no Maitreya after you.

Wumen's verse:

> Words cannot express things.  
> Speech does not convey the spirit.  
> Swallowed by words, you are lost.  
> Blocked by phrases, you are bewildered.

## Modern Reading

The monk is asking a large question. He wants the essence — the innermost meaning of the entire transmission, from Bodhidharma crossing the mountains to wherever Zen has arrived now. What does it all come down to? What is the heart of this?

Zhaozhou turns and points to a tree. Not metaphorically. He points to the cypress tree standing in the garden in front of them. Right there. Now.

## Commentary

The monk wants the answer in words, at the size of the question. Bodhidharma came from the West carrying something enormous — a living transmission, a way of seeing, ten thousand years of inheritance. Surely the answer must match the weight of the question.

Zhaozhou says: cypress tree.

The monk has come carrying a question, hoping to trade it for an explanation. That is the attachment Zhaozhou is cutting. Not the question — questions are fine. The attachment to receiving the answer in a form the mind can carry away and use.

Notice what the cypress tree offers. It does not explain. It does not refer to something else. It does not build toward a conclusion. It is exactly what it is, standing where it stands, indifferent to being the answer.

This is the teaching on non-attachment that cuts deepest: not the instruction to let go of objects, identities, or outcomes. It is the invitation to stop requiring the teaching to come in a form you recognize.

The cypress tree is not a riddle. It is not a symbol. Zhaozhou is not being clever. He is pointing at the ordinary fact of a tree and inviting the monk to stop looking past it for the meaning.

The meaning is here. In this room. In this breath. No less than Bodhidharma's entire crossing.

## Socratic Prompts

1. When you receive an answer that is not in the form you expected, what do you do with it? Think of a time someone showed you something directly and you kept looking for the explanation behind the thing. What were you refusing to see?

2. Right now, what is the most ordinary thing in your field of awareness — a sound, a surface, the quality of the light? What would it mean if this, exactly as it is, were the teaching you came looking for?
