## Original Passage

In the seventh month of the year, Huineng told his disciples he would depart.

One of his disciples began to weep.

Huineng said:

> For whom are you weeping? If you weep because you fear I do not know where I am going, that is not so — I know. If you weep because you think I am not coming back, that is also not the right way to see it. Your original nature does not come and does not go, does not arise and does not cease.

> Good friends, do not be confused. Do not be agitated. What I have given you, keep it. Keep it not as a possession but as a practice — living, moving, adapting. Each person, sitting down, should look into the nature of the mind.

> What is the use of a teaching that does not reach your daily life?

The disciples asked: "After you are gone, whom shall we rely upon for the dharma?"

Huineng said: "Rely upon the dharma. Do not rely upon me."

He sat quietly and passed.

## Modern Reading

The final lesson of a teacher who spent his life refusing to be a teacher's statue. After him, there is nothing to lean on — no robe, no transmission, no lineage to shelter under. There is only the practice, which is only the life.

## Commentary

This is where the text ends. But Huineng's instruction is not to treat this as an ending.

What is the use of a teaching that does not reach your daily life? That question is the whole of it — not a rhetorical flourish, not a closing exhortation. An actual question, addressed to you, today, at the completion of thirty sessions.

Sumi ink dries immediately. The stroke is made and it is done — it cannot be revised, and the paper absorbs it fully. What remains is not the gesture but the mark. What you carry from thirty days of practice is not a set of ideas about Zen. It is, at best, a slightly different way of picking up your bowl. A moment of hesitation before you rush. A breath taken before the habitual reaction. Small things, unremarkable to anyone watching. Yours entirely.

There is no graduation from this. Huineng's disciples were confused by his death because they had been leaning on his presence, and his whole life had been telling them not to do that. The practice does not conclude with the 30th session. It either continues into tomorrow — into the chopping and carrying and washing and walking — or it does not, and the thirty days were just interesting.

Rely upon the dharma. The dharma is not a text. It is the instruction already present in a wet bowl, in an unanswered koan, in the ordinary mind that meets the Tuesday morning as it is.

The ink dries. The practice continues. You already know what to do.
