## Original Passage

> Good friends, what is meant by 坐禅 — sitting meditation? In this school, it means no obstacle and no impediment in any situation. Outwardly, not to set the mind in motion toward any good or evil experience — this is 坐, sitting. Inwardly, to see one's original nature and remain undisturbed — this is 禅, meditation.
>
> Good friends, what is 禅定 — dhyana? Outwardly, to be free of form is dhyana. Inwardly, not to be disturbed is stillness. Outwardly, if you attach to form, the inner mind is disturbed. If you are outwardly free of form, the inner nature is undisturbed.
>
> The original nature is by itself pure, by itself still. It is only because we encounter circumstances and think upon them that disturbance arises. One who sees one's original nature — sitting or walking, standing or lying — is practicing dhyana at all times.

## Modern Reading

Huineng breaks the container. Sitting meditation is not about sitting. The posture is not the thing — the quality of attention is the thing. Walking is sitting. Standing is sitting. Carrying water is sitting. If you have understood the teaching only on the cushion, you have understood it in a protected laboratory and not yet in the world. The practice question for today is not "how do I meditate while walking?" It is: "what would it mean for walking itself to be the complete thing?"

## Commentary

Most people who come to formal practice eventually ask: how do I carry this into my day?

Huineng's answer is blunt. You are asking the wrong question.

There is no "this" to carry out. The original nature is not something you cultivate on the cushion and then try to preserve as you move through traffic. It does not require the cushion to be active. It is active while you walk, while you eat, while you wait for a reply that does not come.

What the cushion does is remove the noise temporarily so you can locate what was always there. The point is not to live on the cushion. The point is to locate it once, clearly, so you can recognize it when it appears elsewhere.

And it always appears elsewhere. It is appearing right now — in the specific weight of your body in this seat, the temperature of the air, the sound this room makes when you stop filling it with commentary.

走路就是修行. Walking is itself the practice.

Today's session is this: whatever you do for the next ten minutes — whether you walk, sit, or simply breathe — do it as though the doing itself is the complete teaching. Not preparation. Not warm-up. Not the ordinary thing before the real thing.

This is the real thing.

## Socratic Prompts

1. Think of a walk you took recently — to a kitchen, to a meeting, down a street. Were you in that walk, or were you somewhere else entirely? Be specific: where were you?
2. Huineng says the original nature is "by itself pure, by itself still." Not achieved — already present. Is there any part of your practice life where you actually believe this, versus where you are still trying to get somewhere?
3. Your body is in a specific position right now. Without shifting it — what does it feel like from the inside? Weight, pressure, temperature, ease or tension? Name four things you actually feel, not four things you generally know about bodies.
4. "It is only because we encounter circumstances and think upon them that disturbance arises." Think of the last time you were disturbed. What was the circumstance, and what was the thinking on top of it? Can you separate the two?
5. If you could not distinguish between "formal practice" and "everything else," what would change about how you move through tomorrow?
6. You will stand up from this session. The first three steps you take — are you willing to take them as complete practice, not transition? What would make that possible or impossible?
7. What is the quality of attention you bring to tasks you consider important versus tasks you consider ordinary? Is there a version of the ordinary task that is equally worthy of that quality?
