## Original Passage

*Fresh translation from Classical Chinese.*

The great Way has no gate.
There are a thousand paths to it.
Pass through this barrier —
and you walk the universe alone.

---

Buddhism takes mind as its essential teaching.
The gateless barrier is the single entrance.
Those who do not pass through this gate
and those who do not see this nature —
they are ghosts clinging to grass and trees.

What is the gateless barrier?
This one word: *Mu.*
This is the barrier of the Zen school.
One who has passed through it
does not only meet Zhaozhou face to face —
one walks hand in hand with all the patriarchs,
eyebrows tangled with theirs,
seeing with the same eyes, hearing with the same ears.
Is this not a great matter?

Those who wish to pass this barrier:
work on it with your whole body,
three hundred and sixty bones and joints,
eighty-four thousand pores —
gather that great doubt into one mass.
Work at it day and night.
Do not interpret it as nothingness.
Do not interpret it as having or not having.
It is like swallowing a red-hot iron ball:
you cannot spit it out.
All former delusions fall away.
After a long time, inside and outside become one.
You know it the way a mute person knows a dream —
known only from within.

## Modern Reading

Wumen wrote this preface in 1228, compiling forty-eight cases as a single gate of entry into Zen. His framing is precise: the gate has no gate. There is no technique that gets you through, no accumulation of correct understanding. The barrier is exactly one word — Mu — and it is both the gate and the passing through.

## Commentary

What Wumen is pointing at is not a puzzle to be solved. He is very clear about this: do not interpret Mu as nothingness, do not interpret it as existence versus non-existence. Every interpretation is a step to the side. The work is to hold the question with the whole body until the boundary between the one asking and the thing asked dissolves.

This sounds extreme, and in a monastic context it could take years of uninterrupted effort. But the underlying structure of the instruction is available to anyone: there is a particular kind of question that cannot be answered by thinking more clearly. You can turn it over, look at it from different angles, apply greater rigor — and none of that produces the thing. What Wumen calls "great doubt" is not confusion. It is the specific quality of attention that comes when you stop expecting a conceptual resolution and simply hold the not-knowing as clearly as possible.

The image of the red-hot iron ball is exact. You cannot think your way around it. You cannot set it down. The only movement possible is through.

## Socratic Prompts

1. Is there a question in your life that you have been turning over conceptually for a long time — a question that thinking harder has not resolved? What does it feel like to hold that question without reaching for an answer?
2. *Koan: "The great Way has no gate." — What were you looking for a gate to?*
